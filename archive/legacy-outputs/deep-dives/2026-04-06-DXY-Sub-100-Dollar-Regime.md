## A. Current Setup

**The Move**: DXY broke below 100 on Monday April 6, trading at 99.81-100.14. The last time DXY was comfortably below 100 was in late 2024. The break represents a -3.3% decline from the baseline of ~103.5 — a significant intraday/weekly FX move.

**Immediate Driver**: Ceasefire proposal triggered risk-on USD selling (de-escalation = less safe-haven demand for USD). EUR/USD surged from ~1.080 to 1.15 (+6.5%) — the single largest catalyst (EUR is 57% of DXY weight).

**But there's a deeper story** — the DXY move is not entirely explained by the ceasefire. Multiple structural forces have been pushing dollar lower:
1. **Fed rate at 3.5-3.75%** (vs. earlier assumptions of 4.75-5.00%) — U.S. rate differential vs. EUR narrowing
2. **De-dollarization trend** — Central bank FX reserve diversification away from USD (ongoing structural)
3. **US current account deficit** — persistent structural USD headwind
4. **Iran War geopolitics** — Ironically, the US engaging in another Middle East war is eroding some of the "American exceptionalism" premium

## B. What DXY Below 100 Means — Historical Context

DXY was established in 1973 at 100. Below 100 means the USD has weakened from its baseline.

**DXY Below 100 Historically**:
- 1992-1997: Dollar weakness as US trade deficits widened; ended with Asian financial crisis
- 2003-2008: Post-dot-com USD decline; major commodity supercycle (gold, oil, EM)
- 2020-2023: COVID-driven dollar weakness, then recovery
- **2024-2026**: Dollar peaked in late 2024 on aggressive Fed hikes, then declined as cuts began

**Pattern in DXY sub-100 regimes**:
- Gold consistently outperforms (commodities generally)
- Emerging markets typically outperform US equities
- International developed (EFA) outperforms SPY
- Inflation tends to remain elevated (imported goods cost more for US consumers)

**Current DXY context**: DXY reached lows around 100 in 2025 as the Fed began cutting, then briefly recovered to 103+ during the Iran War safe-haven demand period. The break below 100 on April 6 may be:
- (a) A temporary spike below 100 driven by ceasefire news that reverses if escalation resumes
- (b) The beginning of a sustained sub-100 regime as war premium fades and Fed remains on hold

## D. Macro Context — Why Dollar Might Stay Weak

### Structural Drivers of USD Weakness (Persistent)

1. **Fed Rate Trajectory**: Fed at 3.5-3.75% with one more cut expected in 2026. ECB may hold or cut slower. Rate differential narrowing = EUR stronger vs USD. Institutional forecasts: DXY low-to-mid 90s by December 2026.

2. **US Fiscal Deficit**: US running 6-7% of GDP deficit. Dollar funding premium may compress as supply of Treasuries increases. Foreign buyers less enthusiastic at lower real yields.

3. **De-dollarization**: BRICS+ countries accelerating non-USD trade settlements (oil, commodities). Saudi Arabia accepting CNY for some oil sales. Russia/China bilateral trade in local currencies. This is slow but structural — reduces global demand for USD.

4. **War Perception**: America's global standing as "safe haven" is being questioned when it's a party to a major regional war. Portfolio diversification away from USD-denominated assets is accelerating among sovereign wealth funds.

### Cyclical Driver (Temporary)

5. **Iran Ceasefire De-escalation**: The immediate catalyst. If ceasefire fails, USD recovers 2-3% within days. If ceasefire succeeds, USD may consolidate sub-100 but not fall dramatically further (war premium = both ways).

### The Tension

The ceasefire scenario = DXY stays weak (risk-on, less safe haven demand), but also = growth recovery = risk-on assets rise = some demand for USD returns. Net: DXY stays in 98-103 range.

The escalation scenario = DXY recovers to 101-104 on safe-haven demand, but then resumes decline as Fed cuts eventually and fiscal deficit widens.

**Long-term view**: DXY is likely in a gradual secular decline from 2025 highs toward 90-95 range by 2027 regardless of ceasefire outcome. The war just interrupted and temporarily reversed this trend.
